## 2. The Problem

### 2.1 Traditional Brick-and-Mortar Limitations

**Entertainment & Food Service:**
- High fixed costs (rent, build-out, long-term leases)
- Geographic lock-in (can't follow demand)
- Stale experiences (expensive to update)
- Underutilized capacity (empty during slow periods)

**Small Business Owners:**
- Tied to single location
- Can't relocate without abandoning investment
- Limited to franchise model for multi-location
- High barrier to entry (build-out costs)

**Housing Crisis:**
- Affordable housing shortage
- Cookie-cutter public housing lacks dignity
- People can't take housing when they move
- Modular housing experiments fail due to lack of infrastructure

### 2.2 Existing "Solutions" Fall Short

**Food Trucks:**
- Limited capacity (2-4 people)
- Vehicle maintenance mixed with business equipment
- No permanent docking option
- Cramped workspace

**Traditional Escape Rooms:**
- Expensive build-out, permanent commitment
- Can't rotate themes easily
- Limited to single location
- High sunk cost if concept fails

**Shipping Container Conversions:**
- One-off custom builds (expensive, no standardization)
- No infrastructure to dock into
- Proprietary systems (not interoperable)
- Treated as "temporary" by codes (limited uses)

**Modular Housing Attempts:**
- No standard docking system
- Expensive custom engineering per project
- Permitting nightmares
- No business model to fund R&D

### 2.3 The Core Issue: Lack of Universal Docking Infrastructure

The problem isn't the containers - it's the absence of:
- Standardized connection points
- Network of compatible locations
- Open specifications enabling competition
- Proven operational model

**We need the "USB port" for physical spaces.**

## 3. The Solution

### 3.1 The Obsidian Portal Concept

**Core Idea:** Modular shipping containers that dock into standardized infrastructure, enabling:
- Rapid theme/concept changes (entertainment)
- Geographic flexibility (food service, retail)
- True ownership + mobility (housing)

**Three-Phase Vision:**

**Phase 1: Entertainment (Escape Rooms, D&D Venues, Mystery Dinners)**
- Prove modularity commercially
- Generate revenue
- Establish first hub locations
- Build operator community

**Phase 2: Food Service & Retail**
- Expand to commercial applications
- Test higher-intensity utilities (kitchens)
- Grow hub network
- Develop vendor mobility

**Phase 3: Affordable Housing**
- Apply proven infrastructure to residential
- Open standard enables competition
- Portable homeownership model
- Scale to address housing crisis

### 3.2 Why Shipping Containers?

- Globally standardized (ISO specifications)
- Proven durability (ocean transport, stacking)
- Available worldwide (used market)
- Existing handling equipment
- Understood by regulators/inspectors
- Modular by design

### 3.3 The Open Standard Approach

**Not a proprietary system - an open standard that enables:**
- Multiple manufacturers competing on quality/price
- Hub operators compete on location/amenities
- Containers dock at ANY compatible hub
- Vendor mobility without franchise fees
- Network effects benefit everyone

**Like:**
- Shipping containers themselves (ISO standard)
- USB connectors (universal compatibility)
- Electrical outlets (standardized globally)

### 3.4 The Network Effect

**With 1 hub:** Limited value (only works there)  
**With 10 hubs:** Vendors can relocate between cities  
**With 50+ hubs:** National mobility, seasonal optimization  
**With international adoption:** Follow demand globally

Each new hub increases value for ALL participants.

## 4. Technical Specification

### 4.1 Container Design

**Physical:**
- 20' shipping container (19' interior x 7.5' x 7.5')
- Front end: Full-width doors (7'8" opening)
- Rear end: Solid wall + utility connection panel
- French cleat system (modular interior panels)
- Reinforced for lifting at both ends
- Insulated to R-value [NEEDS RESEARCH]

**Utility Panel (Rear of Container):**
- NEMA 14-50 electrical inlet (50A/240V)
- ¾" GHT water connection (RV standard)
- 3" wastewater outlet (RV dump standard)
- (4) Cat6 ethernet ports
- 12" HVAC duct coupling (supply + return)
- Optional: Propane quick-disconnect

**Interior:**
- French cleat mounting rails (both long walls)
- 4'x7' swappable wall panels
- Integrated wiring channels (top of cleats)
- ADA-compliant clearances [NEEDS VERIFICATION]

### 4.2 Hub Dock Infrastructure

**Utility Block (at far end of dock pad):**
- Based on RV pedestal design (proven, off-the-shelf)
- Grid power connection + metering
- Municipal water + pressure regulation
- Sewer connection
- Network distribution
- HVAC condenser unit (mini-split outdoor section)
- Optional: Natural gas or propane

**Dock Position:**
- Level concrete pad
- Container parking guides/markers
- Covered walkway to building (weather protection)
- Accessible pathway (ADA compliant)
- Emergency access clearances

**Configuration Options:**
- Linear (containers in a row)
- Radial (hub-and-spoke around central building)
- Clustered (mixed arrangements)

### 4.3 Mobile/Truck Configuration

**Utilities Module (between cab and container):**
- Diesel generator (8-10 kW)
- Battery bank (10-15 kWh lithium)
- Solar panels (roof-mounted, supplemental)
- Fresh water tank (50-75 gallons)
- Gray water tank (50-75 gallons)
- Propane tanks (2x 40lb, automatic changeover)
- Rooftop HVAC unit (15,000 BTU)
- Cellular data modem

**Truck Configuration:**
- [CAB] → [UTILITIES MODULE] → [CONTAINER]
- Container rear connects to utilities module
- Container front (doors) accessible at truck rear
- Standard commercial chassis (non-CDL operation)

**Self-Sufficient Operation:**
- 8-12 hours on battery/generator
- Limited water use (no dishwashing)
- Simplified menu (food service)
- Works for festivals, private events, remote locations

### 4.4 Connection Standards (RV Industry Proven)

All connections use existing RV/food truck standards:
- Electrical: NEMA 14-50 (same as Tesla charger, large RVs)
- Water: Garden hose thread (GHT) with regulator
- Wastewater: 3" RV dump connection
- Propane: ACME/POL quick-disconnect
- HVAC: Standard 12" round duct
- Data: Cat6 ethernet (only non-RV element)

**Why RV standards?**
- 50+ years of proven reliability
- Parts available everywhere
- Every RV shop can service
- Electricians/plumbers know them
- Weatherproof, mobile-rated
- Much cheaper than custom engineering

### 4.5 Use Case Configurations

**Entertainment (Escape Rooms):**
- Utilities: Power, data, HVAC
- No water/sewer needed (use building restrooms)
- Lower power requirements (lights, props, A/V)
- Focus on theming and experience

**Food Service:**
- Utilities: Power, water, wastewater, HVAC, data
- Optional: Propane/natural gas
- Grease trap management [NEEDS RESEARCH]
- Health department compliance [NEEDS RESEARCH]
- Higher power requirements [NEEDS VERIFICATION - 50A enough?]

**Residential (Future):**
- Utilities: Full service (power, water, sewer, HVAC, data)
- Hot water heater
- Residential-grade finishes
- Building code compliance [NEEDS RESEARCH]
- Long-term durability requirements

## 7. Open Standard

### 7.1 Philosophy

**"The standard defines the DOCK, not the capabilities."**

The Obsidian Portal Standard ensures physical and utility compatibility, but does NOT dictate:
- Interior design/theming
- Business model
- Quality/price point
- Specific equipment

**Anyone can:**
- Build containers to the standard
- Operate a hub with compatible docks
- Manufacture components
- Compete on quality/service/price

**The standard enables competition, not monopoly.**

### 7.2 Core Specification (v1.0 Draft)

**Container Requirements:**
- Dimensions: 20' x 8' x 8.5' (ISO standard container)
- Rear utility panel with connections:
  - NEMA 14-50 electrical inlet (50A/240V)
  - ¾" GHT water inlet with regulator
  - 3" wastewater outlet (RV standard)
  - (4) Cat6 RJ45 ports (gigabit capable)
  - 12" HVAC duct coupling
  - [Optional] Propane quick-disconnect
- Front: Full-width access (doors, opening, etc.)
- Weatherproof when all connections sealed
- Liftable/transportable per ISO container specs
- Minimum interior height: 7'6"
- ADA considerations: [NEEDS SPECIFICATION]

**Dock Requirements:**
- Utility block providing:
  - 50A/240V minimum electrical service
  - Potable water at 40-80 PSI
  - Wastewater connection (gravity or pumped)
  - Gigabit ethernet
  - HVAC supply (ducted or compatible with rooftop unit)
  - [Optional] Natural gas or propane
- Level surface for container
- Accessible walkway
- Connection point at rear of dock pad
- Emergency vehicle access
- [Specific dimensional/clearance requirements TBD]

**Optional Extensions:**
- Higher amperage (100A, 200A for food/residential)
- Hot water supply
- Grease trap access (food service)
- Fire suppression connection

## 8. Social Impact Vision

### 8.1 The Housing Application

**The Problem:**
- 11+ million affordable housing units needed in US
- Traditional construction too slow/expensive
- Modular housing experiments fail (no infrastructure)
- People lack mobility when circumstances change

**The Obsidian Portal Solution:**
- Proven dock infrastructure (from commercial use)
- Standardized residential containers
- True homeownership (own container, rent dock)
- Geographic mobility (move container to new hub)

**How It Works:**
- Resident owns or finances their container-home
- Docks at radial housing hub
- Pays monthly for: land lease, utilities, amenities
- Can relocate container to different hub if needed
  - Job relocation
  - Family circumstances
  - Desired lifestyle change
  - Housing market shifts

**Benefits:**
- Affordability (container < traditional construction)
- Dignity (personalized, not institutional)
- Mobility (follow opportunities)
- Ownership (build equity, not just rent)
- Community (radial hub design fosters connection)

### 8.2 Economic Mobility for Small Business Owners

**Current Problem:**
- Entrepreneurs tied to single location
- Relocation means starting over
- Franchise model: pay fees forever, no ownership
- Success in one market doesn't transfer

**Obsidian Portal Solution:**
- Own your business container
- Operate at any compatible hub
- Relocate between cities
- Seasonal optimization (summer beach, winter ski town)

**Example:**
Food vendor builds successful taco concept in Austin:
- Owns container-restaurant (~$50k investment)
- Operates at Austin hub (paying monthly dock fee)
- Opportunity arises in Denver
- Ships container to Denver hub (~$4k)
- Same business, new market, in days not months
- No franchise fees, no starting over

### 8.3 Funding Model: Commerce → Housing

**The Traditional Problem:**
- Housing nonprofits can't fund R&D
- Government grants slow and political
- Venture capital wants returns, not social impact
- Affordable housing "not profitable"

**Our Approach:**
- Entertainment business generates revenue (escape rooms, events)
- Food service business generates more revenue
- Profits fund housing R&D
- Commercial applications prove infrastructure
- Then transition proven system to housing
- Open standard prevents profit-extraction

**Why This Works:**
- Entertainment/food are profitable (fund development)
- Same infrastructure serves housing (no separate R&D)
- Commercial use de-risks technology
- Regulatory acceptance (proven safe/viable)
- Supply chain established (multiple manufacturers)
- Network effects already in place

### 8.4 Theory of Change

**Year 1-3: Prove Commercial Viability**
- Entertainment and food hubs in 3-5 cities
- 50+ containers operating
- Standard adopted by early operators
- Revenue covering costs + profit

**Year 3-5: Scale Commercial Network**
- 20+ cities with hubs
- 500+ containers in operation
- Multiple hub operators (not just us)
- Vendor mobility proven (people moving between hubs)
- Manufacturing scaled (competitive market)

**Year 5-7: Residential Pilot**
- First housing hub using same infrastructure
- 20-50 residential units
- Partnership with housing authority/nonprofit
- Building code approval in pilot jurisdiction
- Prove concept: people actually want this

**Year 7-10: Scale Housing**
- Multiple cities approve residential use
- 100s of housing units deployed
- Market develops (manufacturers, hub operators)
- Advocacy for zoning/code changes nationally
- Replicable model for other communities

**Year 10+: Systemic Change**
- Thousands of portable housing units
- Normalized concept (like mobile homes, but not stigmatized)
- International adoption
- Housing mobility = economic mobility
- Small businesses + housing using same infrastructure

### 8.5 Measures of Success

**Commercial Phase:**
- Number of hubs operating
- Number of containers in circulation
- Vendor satisfaction (would they recommend?)
- Economic viability (profitable operations)
- Standard adoption (competing manufacturers)

**Housing Phase:**
- Units deployed
- Tenant satisfaction
- Affordability metrics (rent < 30% income)
- Mobility events (people actually moving containers)
- Building code acceptances (jurisdictions)
- Replication (other communities adopting)

**Ultimate Impact:**
- Affordable housing units created
- Small businesses enabled
- Economic mobility increased
- Community resilience (housing responds to need)
